The Problem:

94% of deep tech startups fail not because the technology doesn't work, but because:

  • They solve problems nobody has
  • They design for technical performance, not user experience
  • They ignore the humans in the loop (operators, regulators, customers)
  • They iterate in the lab, not with real users

The Solution:

Human-Centered Design (HCD) puts users at the center of innovation from Day 1:

  • Start with empathy (understand real human needs)
  • Ideate rapidly (generate 100 ideas in 30 minutes)
  • Prototype quickly (test with cardboard before CAD)
  • Iterate constantly (feedback loops with actual users)

Why This Matters for Deep Tech:

Unlike consumer apps, deep tech faces unique HCD challenges:

  • Users are experts (nuclear operators, satellite engineers, quantum researchers)
  • Regulators are stakeholders (NRC, FAA, EURATOM need usability proofs)
  • Buyers aren't users (procurement teams buy, engineers use)
  • Systems are complex (300+ touchpoints from lab to deployment)

  1. Apply Design Thinking to hardware-heavy, regulated deep tech
  2. Run user research with small, specialized audiences (<50 potential customers)
  3. Design for both technical performance AND human usability
  4. Navigate regulatory requirements through user-centered documentation
  5. Build MVPs that humans actually want to use (not just technically optimal)

1. The Human-Centered Design Approach

HCD is an approach that puts the human perspective at the center of every step in the problem-solving process. Its core goal is to ensure the final solution is desirable, usable, accessible, and beneficial for the end-users.

  • Inspiration: Deeply immerse in the lives of the people you are designing for to build empathy and uncover unmet needs.
  • Ideation: Brainstorm a wide range of possible solutions based on user insights and create low-fidelity prototypes quickly.
  • Implementation: Refine the concepts, develop the solution, and take it to market, continually gathering feedback and iterating.

2.The Design Thinking Framework

Design Thinking is a tactical, non-linear, and iterative framework for solving complex, ill-defined problems by balancing user needs with technical feasibility and business viability. The widely used Hasso Plattner Institute of Design at Stanford (d.school) model has five stages:

  • Empathize: Conduct research (interviews, observations) to gain an empathic understanding of the user's needs and motivations.
  • Define: Synthesize research findings into a clear, human-centered problem statement.
  • Ideate: Challenge assumptions and generate a wide array of innovative ideas using brainstorming and other creative techniques.
  • Prototype: Build tangible, low-cost representations of selected ideas to test them with users.
  • Test: Gather user feedback on the prototypes to refine and improve the solution through continuous iteration.

The Power of "How Might We" (HMW)

What HMW Does:

  • Reframes problems as opportunities
  • Opens up solution space (doesn't prescribe answers)
  • Invites collaboration ("we", not "I")
  • Assumes solutions are possible ("how", not "if")

Bad Problem Statements:

âŒ "We need to sell more quantum computers"
   (That's your goal, not a user problem)

âŒ "Nuclear power is safe and we need to convince people"
   (That's advocacy, not design)

âŒ "Climate change is happening"
   (Too broad, not actionable)

Good HMW Statements:

âœ… "How might we help pharmaceutical companies discover drugs 10Ã— faster using quantum simulation?"
   (Specific user, specific outcome, clear value)

âœ… "How might we help data center operators reduce costs by 40% using Small Modular Reactors?"
   (Specific user, specific job, clear metric)

âœ… "How might we help mayors show voters their city is prepared for climate change?"
   (Specific user, emotional job, political context)
